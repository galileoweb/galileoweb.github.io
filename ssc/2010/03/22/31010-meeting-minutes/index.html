March 22, 2010 Karin Lau Meeting Minutes

SAN FRANCISCO UNIFIED SCHOOL DISTRICT

Galileo Academy of Science and Technology

1150 Francisco Street, San Francisco, CA  94109

Room 210 at 3:30 P.M.

Location and Time of Meeting

School Site Council Meeting-March 10, 2010

X  Agenda Attached

X  Attendance/sign-in attached

Present:  Vicki Pesek, Ben Chun, Stan DeBella, Doug Page, Bettie Grinnell, Mele Lau-Smith, Audrey Ricci, Leland Wong, Mark Huang, Jennifer Zhang, Karin Lau, Nancy Lambert, Katie Pringle

Absent (Excused): Kathleen Golata, Linda Liu

X  Minutes of the previous meetings approved

Decision Made/Issues Reviewed

I.  Call to Order

– B. Grinnell, SSC chair, called the meeting to order at 3:37pm.  There were ten elected members present; a quorum was established.  The chair welcomed everyone to the meeting.

II.  Approval of 2/20/2010 Meeting Minutes

– S. DeBella moved to approve the minutes as presented.  M. Lau seconded the motion, and all voted in favor of approving the minutes.

III.  Old Business

1.  School Programs Updates

– V. Pesek will update the school programs when she talks about the preliminary budget proposal in IV-New Business, #1.

2.  WASC Accreditation Process Updates

– Z. Duskin is on a WASC visiting committee to accredit a school in Davis.  N. Lambert spoke on her behalf.  The school faculty and staff have met several times as focus groups (by WASC categories) and home groups (by departments).  At the home groups’ last meeting, they looked at the existing Expected School-wide Learning Results (ESLRs) to see what components are missing, what implications for teaching these ESLRs have, and how these could be shared with students and families in meaningful ways.

– It is beneficial for administrators and teachers to visit other schools as visiting committee members; the experience will make the whole process less intimidating.  Hence, N. Lambert will visit Skyline High School (in Oakland) and B. Chun, who is in training, will soon visit a school in Vallejo.  He was on a WASC team a few years back and recalled his experiences as a reviewer:  looking through the school’s evidences, usually gathered in 2-inch thick binders, to determine if their claims of activities were factual.  N. Lambert cited that a reviewer had to prepare a report before the actual visit.  Instead of writing the report from scratch, he/she could get excerpts from the evidences.

— B. Chun questioned the role of the School Site Council in the WASC accreditation process and how they could contribute to the writing of the WASC action plan.  Maybe prior to the actual writing, Z. Duskin had to formulate a survey to help gather parents’ and students’ comments and thoughts.  Because the template of the action plan is aligned with the Balanced Scorecard, Z. Duskin might not need to reinvent writing the report.  It would be ideal if the District would adopt the WASC report as the Balanced Scorecard; therefore, all the schools will only have to be concerned with one document.

*Point Person:  Z. Duskin

Proposed Action:  Two chapters of the WASC report will have been written by April 2010.

3.  Recycling and Composting Regulations/Updates

– K. Pringle has been in constant communication with Eiei to get the recycling and composting program off the ground.  K. Pringle approached two teachers to see if they were interested in sponsoring the recycling/composting club, but they declined the offer.  K. Pringle will continue the search for a sponsor.

— The content in the big blue bin in the hallway eventually makes it to the recycling dumpster.  Before a club is formed, each classroom will have to bring their own recycling materials to the dumpster.

— The Department of Environment (DOE) is happy to come to Galileo to present the assembly on composting.  The department’s website has a myriad of resources/links about green school menus, green school programs to implement, etc.  There is also a bond measure of up to $100,000 to green the school courtyard.  The grant template has been sent to Eiei, who will be working with K. Pringle on a proposal.  The proposal must have some sustainability in place and maintenance by the school site.  The custodial staff can receive $200 stipend per year for their involvement.

— PG&E will suspend their one-panel installation grant program because of its inability to gather sufficient data.  Instead, they will increase the amount of grants, but select fewer schools; therefore, more panels can be installed and more data can be collected.  When a school is selected and panels are installed, the solar panels must be visible so that PG&E can use them for their PR campaign.  Galileo is wait-listed.

4.  SCE Funded Classroom Technology Updates

— K. Pringle will seek B. Chun’s assistance in the creation of an inventory process to keep an accurate account of all the old and new equipment on site.  It is even more crucial now to devise a system because of future budget constraints, which impede any buying power.  Currently, there isn’t a process in place and these components, which are ordering, receiving, tagging, logging-in, deliveries, are all over the places and involve several departments.  Therefore, some of the information might fall through the cracks and/or sketchy.

— The next step is for B. Chun to design a program/database, so that after K. Pringle sorts through all the records, she can take an accurate inventory of what we have.  This knowledge will support any future purchasing and enable the school to do so efficiently.

— One component, which is noticeably missing in any records, will be added to the inventory process and that is the return of an item.  If an item is not retuned, then is it stolen or missing.  When an item gets stolen, who is liable for it?  There should be some extenuating circumstances that free the teachers from liability, such as when someone breaks into the car and steals it.  But when an item gets stolen due to the teacher’s negligence, such as leaving the classroom unlocked and the item unattended, then it is up for debate.  Another issue is that teachers should not use the school computers for their personal business.

5.  Calendaring Approved Budget Items for Review

— After reviewing the chart, the council overlooked two items, which should have been revisited at a later date.   The items were J. Taylor’s presentation of his Prop H Arts budget and E. Winterling’s presentation of his Prop H PE budget to the council.  Though a future appearance was not requested at the time, the council would like both department chairs to come in soon to talk about their preliminary budget allocations for 2010/2011.  They should also talked about how their spending pattern this year helps them shape their budget planning for next year.  From here on, departments that get funding, i.e. Prop H Arts, Prop H PE, ELD, should make a presentation to the site council at least once a year.

— M. Lau volunteered to continue updating the chart.  A. Ricci suggested that the chart should be current; delete any items, which have been approved and completed.

*Point Person:  M. Lau-Smith

Proposed Action:  Continue to update the chart

*Point Person:  K. Lau

Proposed Action:  Invite J. Taylor and E. Winterling to the next site council meeting to present their preliminary budget proposals

6.  Filters at Fountains verse Bottled Water

— There isn’t any new development in this agenda item.  K. Pringle will update the council at the next council meeting.

IV.  New Business

1.  2010/2011 Preliminary Budget Proposal

— K. Lau passed out the green and yellow handouts.  One handout lists the Fall Final and Spring Preliminary budget allocations, and the differences between the two for all the different funding sources:

Weighted Student Formula (WSF):  difference is +$8,825

Special Ed-WSF:  difference is -$1,204

Targeted Inst Improvement Block Grant (TIIG):  difference is -$703,661

EIA/SCE:  difference is -$81,287

EIA/LEP:  difference is -$22,887

Prop H Arts:  difference is -$4,968

Prop H PE:  difference is -$9,256

GRANT TOTAL:  difference is -$814,438

— After paying for all the personnel, the school is left with $49,478 in WSF for non-personnel, which is used to buy/pay for supplies, equipment, postage, travel/conference, field trips, substitute days, rental/repairs of equipment, etc.  The current year’s non-personnel budget is $358,974.

— The reason that Galileo didn’t receive any TIIG money is because of our low African American and Latino student population; Galileo has 5% as compared to other high schools, with one small school having 15%.  S. Fewer, board member and a parent, has fought on the school’s behalf to get some of the TIIG money reinstated; we have a good chance of getting back $250,000.  The rationale is that 5% of 2100 enrollees yields the similar quantity as 15% of 900 enrollees.  We won’t know when the money will be allocated to the school and if the preliminary budget planning should include this pending amount.

— The administration will try to balance the preliminary budget without consolidating too many teachers.  But in order to do so, many of the vacant positions will not be re-filled and that will cause the class size to go up next year.  Besides the deep budget cuts, the increased average cost, $3,900 more, of a teacher also plays a big part why 15.25 positions have to be impacted, of which are:

6 teaching positions – will be deleted and not replaced due to 2 resignations and 4                                                 retirements

1 counseling position – will be deleted to balance the budget

4 teaching positions – will be funded by WSF non-personnel money

1 teaching position – will be funded by LEP money

1 para-professional position – will be deleted to balance the budget

2.25 clerical positions – will be deleted to balance the budget

— Before the site council could vote to approve the preliminary budget, B. Chun suggested giving the members more details about the cuts and which departments these eliminated teaching positions came from.  So if most of the cuts were made in the English department, then it would not be equitable.

*Point Persons:  K. Pringle and K. Lau

Proposed Action:  Present the spreadsheet showing where the cuts come from and staffing allocation by departments

2.  School Selection Process

— K. Pringle was appointed to her current assistant principal position by the Leadership, Equity, Achievement and Design (LEAD) department; and therefore, she had bypassed the interview selection process.  That position became vacant after the interview selection process had lapsed.  The school site council could decide to send her through the upcoming interview selection process or elect to appoint her.  In this instance, the principal needed to write a letter with the site council’s support requesting the appointment based on her strengths and effectiveness.  B. Chun moved to appoint K. Pringle to the same position and D. Page seconded the motion.  All voted in favor of the motion.

V.  Open Forum

1.  A. Ricci asked why the swim team was fined $1000 for beginning their practice season too soon.  N. Lambert doubted such occurrence.  She was only aware of one instance where the athletic office penalized two coaches with a fine of $150 for not having a current CPR certification.

S. DeBella moved to adjourn the meeting.  M. Lau-Smith seconded the motion and the meeting was adjourned at 5:12 pm.
