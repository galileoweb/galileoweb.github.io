Chemistry Lesson 1/21/11 – Gas Laws

January 20, 2011 Copernicus Uncategorized

Go to http://crescentok.com/staff/jaskew/ISR/chemistry/class17.htm

1.  What are the two main gases in our atmosphere and what are their respective percentages?

2.  Describe the 4 ways that gases differ from liquids and solids.

3.  In air, the molecules take up only about ____% of the total volume – compared to the individual molecules of a liquid that occupy about ___% of the total space.

4.  Gases exert a ___________, or a force, on any surface with which they are in contact.

5.  What is the equation relating pressure, force and area?

6.  The atmosphere as a whole presses down on Earth’s surface, creating an ___________________  pressure.

7.  How much pressure does 1 square meter of air exert on Earth?  _______ N/m2.    One N/m2 equals 1 _____, the SI unit of pressure.

8.  Standard atmospheric pressure, the typical pressure at ________   _______________ is equal to _______________ Pa.

9.  1 atmosphere, atm = ______ mm Hg = ______ torr = ______________ Pa = ___________ kPa = ______ millibars, mb = ______ lb/in2, psi

10.  In laboratories, a device called a ______________  is often used to measure the pressure of enclosed gases. Although it operates on a principle similar to that of a mercury barometer, one end of the manometer tube is usually _______  to the atmosphere instead of being ___________.

11.  Copy the two digrams of both manometers.    Beneath each drawing, label the one with gas pressure lower than atmospheric pressure and the one with gas pressure higher than atmospheric pressure.

12  Temperature is a measure of the “heat content” or “_____________  _____________” of matter. Temperature changes will cause the ___________ of matter to change.

13.  Tips for working with gas laws:

  • All gas calculations must use __________ temperatures.
  • The conditions ____ oC and ____ atm are referred to as standard temperature and pressure – STP.
  • The volume occupied by one ______ of a gas at STP, ______ liters, is referred to as molar volume.
  • Read the problem to see what ____________ (meaning pressure, volume, or temperature) change.
  • Decide which gas ______  to use and write its equation.
  • Reread the problem to see what question is asked.
  • If needed, ______________ the gas law equation.
  • Plug numbers and units into the equation.

14.  Boyle’s Law states: the ___________ of a fixed quantity of gas maintained at constant temperature is inversely proportional to the ____________.

15.  When two measurements are ______________ proportional, one gets smaller as the other gets bigger.

16.  Write the Boyle’s Law equation.

17.  Charles’ Law states: the ___________ of a fixed amount of gas maintained at constant pressure is directly proportional to its absolute __________________.

18.  When two measurements are __________ proportional, as one changes in size the other undergoes the same size change.19.  Write the Charles’ Law equation.

20.  Boyle’s Law and Charles’ Law can be used in combination when both ____________ and ________________ change. This relationship produces the Combined Gas Law Equation.

21.  Write the Combined Gas Law equation.

22.    Dalton’s Law of Partial Pressures states: the total pressure of a _______________ of gases equals the ______ of the pressures that each would exert if it were present alone.

23.  The pressure exerted by a particular component of a mixture of gases is called the ___________  ____________ of that gas.

24.  Dalton’s Law of Partial Pressures is expressed by what equation?

25.  A gas collected “over water” is a mixture of the gas and ________   __________.

26.  Dalton’s law of partial pressures describes this situation with what equation?

27.  Avogadro’s Hypothesis states: Equal volumes of gases at the same temperature and pressure contain equal numbers of _____________.

28. At 0 oC and 1 atm, ______ L of any gas contains 6.02 X 1023 gas molecules.

29.  Avogadro’s Law states: The volume of a gas maintained at constant temperature and pressure is directly proportional to the number of ________ of the gas.

30.  Write the Avogadro’s Law equation.

31.  An ideal gas is a _______________  gas whose molecules have no volume and no attraction to other molecules. While real gas molecules do have volume and are attracted to other molecules, at common temperatures the difference is so small that it can be ____________.

32.  Write the Ideal-Gas equation.

33.  P is standard pressure in kPa,  V is molar volume,  n is number of ________, T is standard temperature in ___, and R is called the gas ____________.

34.  The value and units of ___ depend on the units of P, V, n, and T.

35.  What are the 2 commonly used values for R?  Give numbers AND units.

Chemistry Standards for Test on Chapters 11 and 12

January 18, 2011 Copernicus Uncategorized

 

Chemistry Standards for Test on Chapters 11 and 12

1. The periodic table displays the elements in increasing atomic number and shows how periodicity of the physical and chemical properties of the elements relates to atomic structure. As a basis for understanding this concept:

b. Students know how to use the periodic table to identify metals, semimetals, nonmetals, and halogens.

c. Students know how to use the periodic table to identify alkali metals, alkaline earth metals and transition metals, trends in ionization energy, electronegativity, and the relative sizes of ions and atoms.  Click here for a diagram summarizing periodic trends (also remember that electronegativity generally increases as you go from left to right in the periodic table, as illustrated on page 362):  http://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/41/PERIODIC_TRENDS.jpg

d. Students know how to use the periodic table to determine the number of electrons available for bonding.

g.* Students know how to relate the position of an element in the periodic table to its quantum electron configuration and to its reactivity with other elements in the table.

h.* Students know the experimental basis for Thomson’s discovery of the electron, Rutherford’s nuclear atom, Millikan’s oil drop experiment, and Einstein’s explanation of the photoelectric effect.

i.* Students know the experimental basis for the development of the quantum theory of atomic structure and the historical importance of the Bohr model of the atom.

j.* Students know that spectral lines are the result of transitions of electrons between energy levels and that these lines correspond to photons with a frequency related to the energy spacing between levels by using Planck’s relationship (E=hv)

2. Biological, chemical, and physical properties of matter result from the ability of atoms to form bonds from electrostatic forces between electrons and protons and between atoms and molecules. As a basis for understanding this concept:

a. Students know atoms combine to form molecules by sharing electrons to form covalent or metallic bonds or by exchanging electrons to form ionic bonds.

b. Students know chemical bonds between atoms in molecules such as H2, CH4, NH3, HCCH2, N2, Cl2, and many large biological molecules are covalent.

c. Students know salt crystals, such as NaCl, are repeating patterns of positive and negative ions held together by electrostatic attraction.

e. Students know how to draw Lewis dot structures.

f.* Students know how to predict the shape of simple molecules and their polarity from Lewis dot structures.

g.* Students know how electronegativity and ionization energy relate to bond formation.

Biology State Standards on Evolution

January 14, 2011 Copernicus Uncategorized

California State Standards on EVOLUTION

7.  The frequency of an allele in a gene pool of a population depends on many factors and may be stable or unstable over time. As a basis for understanding this concept:

  1. Students know why natural selection acts on the phenotype rather than the genotype of an organism. (Example:  Albinism in snakes) aa – albino; Aa – normal; AA – normal
  2. Students know why alleles that are lethal in a homozygous individual may be carried in a heterozygote and thus maintained in a gene pool. (Example:  sickle cell anemia in Africa; Dragonfly p. 348; Turtle p. 262)
  3. Students know new mutations are constantly being generated in a gene pool. (Mistakes/changes in DNA happen in DNA all the time, especially from mutagens like UV rays from the sun.  Most of the time, they are harmful, but sometimes they can give rise to helpful changes/adaptations. Dragonfly p.394; Turtle p.245 & p.323-324)
  4. Students know variation within a species increases the likelihood that at least some members of a species will survive under changed environmental conditions.  (Examples:  peppered moths, Finches on Daphne Major, fork/knife/spoon activity).
  5. * Students know the conditions for Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium in a population and why these conditions are not likely to appear in nature. (know the five conditions; give examples of nonrandom mating, genetic drift, mutation, etc. Dragonfly p.401-402; Turtle p.323-325)
  6. * Students know how to solve the Hardy-Weinberg equation to predict the frequency of genotypes in a population, given the frequency of phenotypes. (Honors only – be able to do calculations with p + q = 1;  p2 + 2pq + q2 = 1)

8.  Evolution is the result of genetic changes that occur in constantly changing environments. As a basis for understanding this concept:

  1. Students know how natural selection determines the differential survival of groups of organisms.  (Examples – giraffes, sickle cell anemia, peppered moths, finches)
  2. Students know a great diversity of species increases the chance that at least some organisms survive major changes in the environment (Examples – peppered moths, finches.  Dragonfly p.380-381; p.386; Turtle p.260)
  3. Students know the effects of genetic drift on the diversity of organisms in a population. (Examples – cheetahs (Turtle book p.325, Amish – website lesson Sickle Cell Anemia, etc.; Turtle p.324-325; Dragonfly p.400)
  4. Students know reproductive or geographic isolation affects speciation. (Examples – Dragonfly book p. 404, 405; Turtle Book p. 264-266)
  5. Students know how to analyze fossil evidence with regard to biological diversity, episodic speciation, and mass extinction. (Especially Mass Extinctions – there were five; what happened to diversity and the number of new species after these mass extinctions? Turtle p. 278-279; Bottom of Dragonfly book p. 435; also Mass Extinction computer lesson; also movies – wolf and whale movie and Extinction movie)
  6. * Students know how to use comparative embryology, DNA or protein sequence comparisons, and other independent sources of data to create a branching diagram (cladogram) that shows probable evolutionary relationships (Embryology – Sickle Cell Anemia Computer Lesson and Turtle Book p.257; Dragonfly book p. 382-386 & p.452-455; Turtle book p.252-257)
  7. * Students know how several independent molecular clocks, calibrated against each other and combined with evidence from the fossil record, can help to estimate how long ago various groups of organisms diverged evolutionarily from one another. (Computer Lab Assignment from 1/31/07 – Tree of Life where you compared the DNA of the opossum, raccoon, and bear.  Dragonfly p.455; Turtle p.255

2.  Sunlight makes _______________ lines, because the sun contains various substances.  It took about one _______________ years until these lines could be explained by ______________  ____________.

3.  Quantum theory deals with _________________ particles.

4.  Who were the six physicists that collaborated to work on quantum theory?

5.  Who was the originator of quantum theory and who formulated the Uncertainty Principle?

6.  Planck experimented with the ________________ of heated materials.  He found that energy was always emitted (released) or absorbed in ____________ units, which he called ___________.  This means that instead of energy being released in a continuous gradient, like the points in Doodle Jump, instead of big discrete jumps, like the points in MegaJump.

7.  Planck came up with his law:  E=hf , where h is a __ __ __ __ __ __ __ __, or a number that never changes.

principle states by precise inequalities that certain pairs of
physical properties, such as position and momentum,
cannot be simultaneously known to a high degree of
__________________. Published by Werner Heisenberg in 1927, the principle means that it is impossible to determine simultaneously
both the position and momentum of an electron or any
other particle with any great degree of _______________ or
__________________.

10. To know the velocity of a quark we must
measure it, and to measure it, we are forced to ____________ it.
The same goes for observing an object’s position.

11.  What did Rutherford demonstrate about the atom? 

12.  What was Niels Bohr’s model of the atom? (3 sentences)
