Chemistry Final Exam Hints!!!

December 12, 2011 Copernicus Uncategorized

1. Physcial changes vs. Chemical changes – often physical changes are things like changes of state, for example, water melting or evaporating is a physical change because it’s still water.  In chemical changes, the chemicals change into something else, i.e. they react.  When looking for chemical properties, therefore, look for words like “react with”, “reactive”, “nonreactive”.

2.  The symbol for Carbon is C.  The symbol for Chromium is Cr.

3.  The chemical formula for CO2 indicates there are one atom of carbon and 2 atoms of oxygen.

4.  Make sure you can do #29 page 82.  Answers:  #29 a) 94 protons, 244-94=150 neutrons, and 94 electrons.    b)  95 protons, 241-95=146 neutrons, and 95 electrons. 

5.  The correct name for MgBr2 is magnesium bromide.  (easy because Mg is in column 2A on the periodic table – for the first two columns (1A and 2A), you don’t need prefixes or roman numerals).  Magnesium is +2 and Bromide is -1 (make sure you write the charges on the top of the columns on the period table), so you need 2 Bromides to balance out the positive 2 charge on Magnesium.

6.  The correct formula for copper (I) oxide is Cu2O.  Notice that the roman number, I, tells you the CHARGE on Cu, NOT the number of atoms.  Since Oxygen has a charge of -2, then you need two Coppers, each with a charge of +1 to balance out the charge on Oxygen of -2. 

7.  Express 675000 in scientific notation.  Answer:  6.75 x 105. 

8.  The measurement 2.1 x 102 grams can also be written in kg:     0.21 kg    (why?  Because there are 1000 grams in a kilogram, so you have to divide grams by 1000 to get to kg).

9.  Using the rules of significant figures, calculate the following:   27.835 – 3.2        Answer:  24.6  (why?  Because the rule in significant figures for addition and subtraction is that you round off to the smallest number of decimal places.  In this case, you had one number, 27.835 that was to the thousandths place, and 3.2 that was to the tenths place, so tenths is the least number of decimal places.  Therefore, you round off to the tenths place.

10.  In the product 2.02 x 0.4567, the number of significant figures is ____.    Answer:  3 (why?  Because the rule with multiplication and division is that you round off to the least number of significant figures.  2.02 has 3 sig figs and 0.4567 has 4 sig figs, so your final answer can only have 3 sig figs.

11.  Make sure you write down in your notes the temperature equations:  K=C+273;  C=K-273; F=1.8C+32; C=(F-32)/1.8      There will be 2 problems using 2 of these equations.

12.  Density = mass/volume, or D=m/V.   Remember that 1 cm3 = 1mL.

13.  One mole of carbon atoms is 6.02 x 1023 atoms.  (why?  One mole of anything is 6.02 x 1023)

14.  One mole of carbon dioxide weighs 44.01 grams.    (why?  Carbon dioxide is CO2.  Molar mass of CO2 (from the periodic table) is 12.01 g + (2 x 16.00 g) = 44.01 grams.)

15.  What is the mass of 3 moles of CO2?    Answer:  3 x 44.01 g = 132.03 g

16.  Know how to figure out molar mass.  Example:  Cu(NO3)2    Answer:  1 Cu + 2N + 6 O = 63.55 g/mol + (2 x 14.01 g/mol) + (6 x 16.00 g/mol) = 187.57 g/mol

17.  3.75 g of CO2 contains how many moles of CO2?   Answer:  0.0852 moles

18.  Calculating empirical formulas from percent composition:  Know how to do a problem like Example 6.14 page 182.  I went over this during class when I taught Chapter 6; a simplified sample problem is in your notebook.  The steps:  change the percents to grams, convert the grams to moles, divide each number of moles by the smallest number of moles, and these numbers are the subscripts for empirical formula of your compound.

19.  Know how to balance equations.  There will be 2 balancing equations problems.  The only way you can do this is to practice problems from Chapter 7. 

20.  It will be helpful to you to write down on your periodic table some of the more common acid formulas from Chapter 4 page 105.   They are hydrochloric acid (HCl), nitric acid (HNO3), and sulfuric acid (HSO4).

21.  You will have to predict the precipitate (solid) like you did for the Chapter 8 test.  I will give you a copy of the solubility rules from page 218 on the Final exam. 

22.  Know the difference between a molecular equation, complete ionic equation, and a net ionic equation (read page 224 if you don’t know).

23.  Be able to classify reactions into precipitation, acid-base, single-replacement, synthesis, and combustion reactions.  This is in your notes in your notebook but also Chapter 8.

24. The equation 2H2O2 -> 2H2O + O2 tells you that 2 moles of H2O2 makes 2 moles of H2O and 1 mole of O2. 

25.  Chapter 9 stuff: 

If you’re given moles and you’re asked to find moles, you use the mole ratio from the equation.  I went over a sample problem in your notes on Chapter 9.

If you’re given grams of one chemical A and you’re asked to find grams of a different chemical B  in the equation, then you have to convert as follows:  grams A to moles A to moles B to grams B.   To change from grams A to moles of A, you use the molar mass of A.  To go from moles of A to moles of B, you use the mole ratio from the balanced chemical equation.  To go from moles of B to grams of B, you use the molar mass of B.   I went over a sample problem in your notes on Chapter 9. 

Percent yield (also called percentage yield):    actual yield/theoretical yield x 100%.  The actual yield would be a number given in the problem; this would be the amount that the scientist actually obtains in the experiment.  Theoretical yield is the amount expected; I will give you this number in the problem. 

Limiting Reagent:  The hardest problem on the test.  I went over this in class; it’s in your Chapter 9 notes.  I summarized Example 9.7 page 273-275 for you.  In summary:  convert the grams of both reactants to moles, divide each number of moles by the smallest number of moles, compare the ratio of moles you calculated for both reactants (your “have” ratio) to the ratio of both reactants based on the balanced chemical equation (your “want” ratio).  The one reactant chemical that you “have” less of is your limiting reagent.  That is the number that you use to find your final grams of product.  At that point it is a moles reactantà moles product (using mole ratio) à grams product (using molar mass of product).  This problem takes practice.  There is one limiting reagent problem out of a total of 50 final exam questions, so I would focus on the knowing the easier ones before stressing out about this one. 

26.  Chapter 10 stuff: 

Be able to define the following Chapter 10 vocabulary words:  temperature, heat, kinetic energy, potential energy, endothermic, exothermic.

Write down the following equation on your periodic table:  ∆E = q + w

Be able to use and manipulate the following equation:  Q = sm∆T, where Q is energy (heat) in Joules, s  is the specific heat capacity in J/g-°C, m is the mass in grams, and ∆T is the change in Temperature in Celsius degrees.  Be able to do #26 on page 319  (note that to solve for s, specific heat capacity, you have to rearrange the equation to s=Q/m∆T).

Be able to do #31 page 319.  I went over this as a Do Now.  It was a homework problem, so some of you wrote it in your Chapter 10 homework, and some of you wrote it in the back of your notebook.

GOOD LUCK!!!!!   Don’t forget to bring your notebook to the final!  Don’t forget to bring your periodic table covered with all of your notes to the final!!
