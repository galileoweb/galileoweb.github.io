Chemistry Lesson – Intermolecular Forces

February 1, 2011 Copernicus Uncategorized

Click here:  http://www.wisc-online.com/objects/ViewObject.aspx?ID=GCH6804

1.  Intermolecular forces exist between __________________________, as opposed to chemical bonds, which are between atoms.

2.  What are the 3 types of intermolecular forces between molecules?

3.  What are the strongest London forces called?

4.  The strongest intermolecular forces molecules are less than ______% as strong as the bonds between atoms.

5.  Intermolecular forces are _____________________________ forces that outweigh the molecules’ kinetic energy and cause the substance to form _______________________ and _______________________ phases instead of gas phase.

6.  Describe two reasons molecules have an uneven distribution of charge.

7.  Dipole-dipole forces result from the attraction of the _________________________ region of one molecule to the __________________________ region of a second molecule.

8.  Draw two molecules with a dipole-dipole attraction.

9.  Molecules of Cl2, Br2 do not have dipoles, i.e. they are not  ___ ___ ___ ___ ___ (you may need to review this in Chapter 12).

10.  Molecules of Cl2 and Br2 do not have permanent dipoles, but at any given time, the electrons in their atoms are ___________________________ distributed.  This uneven distribution of electrons creates an ___________________________________ (“momentary”) dipole.  There is an attraction between these momentary _____________________.

11.  Every atom and molecule experiences these _______________________ forces.  The strength of these forces increases as the _____________________ or __________________________________ increases.    Larger molecules have more ____________________________ and there are larger and more frequent ________________________ formed throughout the molecule.

12.  The boiling point of compounds is when they go from liquid phase into _________ phase.  For molecules to be far apart enough to become gas, their kinetic energy must be greater than all the attractive forces between the molecules, even the weakest ones, called ________________________ forces.  Larger molecules have higher boiling points because there are greater London forces, greater attraction between molecules, less _________________ energy (movement between molecules), a _________________ temperature required to move the liquid into gas phase, and therefore a higher _________________________ point.

Part II:  Which is the dominant force, London, dipole-dipole, or hydrogen bonding in the following molecules and explain why. (no polarity in the molecule = London dispersion & yes polarity in the molecule = dipole-dipole;  Hydrogen bonding is only in molecules that have a Hydrogen bonded to either Nitrogen, Oxygen, or Fluorine.)   If the molecule is symmetrical, then it’s London forces only.  Be careful though, you need to visualize the molecule in 3-D! So draw the Lewis structure, check for electronegativity differences, and see if there’s a net pull in any one direction.

How to draw Lewis structures for molecules that contain no charged atoms

1)    Count the total valence electrons for the molecule: To do this, find the number of valence electrons for each atom in the molecule, and add them up.

2)    Figure out how many octet electrons the molecule should have, using the octet rule: The octet rule tells us that all atoms want eight valence electrons (except for hydrogen, which wants only two), so they can be like the nearest noble gas. Use the octet rule to figure out how many electrons each atom in the molecule should have, and add them up.  The only weird element is boron – it wants six electrons.

3)    Subtract the valence electrons from octet electrons: Or, in other words, subtract the number you found in #1 above from the number you found in #2 above. The answer you get will be equal to the number of bonding electrons in the molecule.

4)    Divide the number of bonding electrons by two: Remember, because every bond has two electrons, the number of bonds in the molecule will be equal to the number of bonding electrons divided by two.

5)    Draw an arrangement of the atoms for the molecule that contains the number of bonds you found in #4 above: Some handy rules to remember are these:

    • Hydrogen and the halogens (Group 7A) bond once.
    • The family oxygen is in (Group 6A) bonds twice.
    • The family nitrogen is in (Group 5A) bonds three times.  So does boron.
    • The family carbon is in (Group 4A) bonds four times.

A good thing to do is to bond all the atoms together by single bonds, and then add the multiple bonds until the rules above are followed.

6)    Find the number of lone pair (nonbonding) electrons by subtracting the bonding electrons (#3 above) from the valence electrons (#1 above). Arrange these around the atoms until all of them satisfy the octet rule: Remember, ALL elements EXCEPT hydrogen (Hydrogen only wants 2) want eight electrons around them, total. Hydrogen only wants two electrons.
