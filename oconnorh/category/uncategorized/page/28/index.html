School Goals – State Standards – Lesson Connection Examples

August 13, 2010 Copernicus Uncategorized

School Goals and Examples of Content Standards & Lessons Related to Goals

 

Biology

School Goal

Content Standard & Lessons
-Creative Critical Thinkers that use available technologies to deepen their understanding BI1.f.  Students know usable energy is captured from sunlight by chloroplasts and is stored through the synthesis of sugar from carbon dioxide (photosynthesis)

Ex. – probeware/Excel experiment analyzing O2/CO2 production

 
-Effective Communicators who can use contemporary platforms and tools to communicate their ideas Ex. – graphing and typing up lab report of  photosynthesis experiment

 

Chemistry

School Goal

Content Standard & Lessons
-Lifelong adaptive learners who know how to learn to use new technologies and teach themselves new skills Ex. – Use probeware for pH, temperature, acid/base conductivity (Chem Standard 5.d)
-Effective Communicators who can write and speak technically… Ex – Typed Lab Report on Moles (Chem Standard 3.b) and Stoichiometry (Chem Standard 3.e)

Biology Syllabus

August 13, 2010 Copernicus Uncategorized

Course Syllabus:  Biology

Instructor:  Ms. O’Connor; email:  oconnorh@galileoweb.org

Textbooks:  Miller & Levine, Biology

Course Description:  Biology will cover the key biological concepts as outlined in the State Science Standards.  The two main goals of the course are to develop a conceptual framework for modern biology and to gain experience and appreciation of biology through experimentation and inquiry.  Students will participate in projects that will develop the following skills:  writing, critical thinking, technology usage, research, time management, cooperation, and presentation skills.

Grading:  Students will earn grades according to the following system:

Tests:               50%

Homework:      15%

Classwork:       20%

Labs:                15%

Participation:     Extra Credit

 Grades are posted on School Loop.  Most assignments are on galileoweb.org/oconnorh.  Ms. O’Connor is available for tutoring on Mondays after school from 3:10-4:40 in Room 450 or lunchtime by appointment.

Expectations:  Students are expected to:

  1. Be on time every day.
  2. Bring all required materials, which include the completed homework assignment, a laboratory notebook, pen, pencil, and some loose binder paper.  In addition, sometimes you will be asked to bring your textbook to class.  Be respectful of themselves, their fellow students, and their teacher by
    1. Listening to students or teachers when they are speaking to the class. 
    2. Listening carefully to all directions, especially laboratory directions to prevent accidents to themselves and others.
    3. Asking for help from fellow students and teachers when necessary.

 

Topics of the Course:

—1st Semester—-
Week 1-3 Introductions, Measurement, Scientific Method, Basic Biochemistry
Week 4-9 Cells
Week 10-11 Respiration/Photosynthesis
Week 12-16 Genetics
Week 17-18 Genetics Project; Review for Final Exam
—2nd Semester—-
Week 19-21 Ecology
Week 22-27 Evolution and Classification of the 6 Kingdoms and major Phyla
Week 28-34 Human Physiology:  Overview, Digestion and Nutrition, Skeletal and Muscular, Endocrine, Nervous, Immune, Reproductive

Synthetic Biology

July 14, 2010 Copernicus Uncategorized

You are a scientist who is trying to figure out how to kill cancer cells more effectively.

Here is a video that will help you understand natural killer cells:  http://www.molecularmovies.com/movies/berry_apoptosis.html.

Natural killer cells have proteins that help them detect cancer cells on their surface.  Once those proteins detect cancer cells, they send protein messages to the inside of the cell.  Then these proteins can send messages to do different things in the cell, maybe interacting with the DNA to make certain proteins, maybe causing the NK cell to leave that cancer cell, maybe increasing the amount of “killing” proteins (granzymes) in the NK cell.

So if you were a scientist that could wave his/her magic wand, what are some changes you would want to make to this cell?

Generate a list.  Now let’s go through the list as a class and analyze their logic and potential.

I’m going to give you a list of proteins, organized into sensors, info processing, and output.  I’ll put you in groups and you will figure out different ways of putting those proteins together that would make new functions in the Natural Killer cell.  Let’s discuss what these “parts” really do in cells.  Here is a link to the protein “parts”:  DNAParts

Now you are going to learn all the steps involved in generating these new combinations of proteins.  These former Lincoln students spent the summer working on the iGEM competition http://2010.igem.org/Team:UCSF  and they had to generate these proteins and combine them.

First you order the DNA that codes for the proteins that you want to combine.  The DNA arrives in a relatively small amount, so you need to amplify it.

Then you need to make sure that the DNA is the right size, so you run a gel.

First you pour a gel.  Then you would take the comb out of the gel and load DNA into the gel.  Watch Crystal doing just that:  http://www.vimeo.com/13523095

Then you need to physically take that DNA out of the gel and purify it out of the gel so you know you have only DNA.

Hannah does gel purification. from Heather O’Connor on Vimeo.

Then you need to put the DNA that codes for your protein into larger piece of DNA that will be taken up by bacterial cells.  Click here to see Crystal spreading her transformed cells on the petri dish.

Click here to see the cells growing in the incubator.

Then you will grow your plate overnight on a plate at 37*C (why 37*?).  All of these cells will have the DNA that you inserted.  But you won’t have very many colonies and therefore not much DNA.  So then you need to pick out those few colonies and inoculate them on a plate,

Watch Crystal plating transformed cells with shaking beads.

Plates in the incubator:  http://www.vimeo.com/13523430

Then you would put those cells in the PCR machine.  The PCR machine has the ability to burst the cells open and only amplify the DNA that you intended.

Then you do a miniprep to purify just the bacteria out of the bacterial cells.

Click here to see Jacinto (with Jun’s assistance) perform a mini-prep of his desired DNA:  http://www.vimeo.com/13746704

Then you would analyze her gel photograph taken after a test digest to try to figure out if she has the right size bands after digesting her DNA with a known restriction enzyme.

Send the DNA for sequencing.

Watch Carmen compare what was sequenced to what she expected.

Watch Lianna do an overview of the whole process from template DNA to DNA parts:  http://www.vimeo.com/13522995.

Biology Lesson 5/18/10 – Analysis of Plants in a Bottle Experiment and your bacteria data

May 17, 2010 Copernicus Uncategorized

Click here for our class data for our Plants in a Bottle Experiment.

Purpose:  To see which bottle design is best for plant growth.

Results:  Look at the data and write a paragraph describing the conditions where the plants grew.

Conclusions: Write a conclusion paragraph describing the best conditions for plant growth and any errors and/or improvements to our experiment.

Bacteria Experiment:

Look at your data table.  Which areas had bacteria?  Which areas didn’t?  Explain your ideas for the differences.  List any errors that you think may have happened.
