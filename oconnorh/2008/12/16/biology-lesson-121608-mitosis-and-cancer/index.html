Biology Lesson 12/16/08 – p53, Introns & Exons, Transposons, HIV

December 16, 2008 Copernicus Uncategorized

Go to http://www.accessexcellence.org/WN/SUA11/arf1297.html

 

  1. The St. Jude researchers discovered that ARF interacts with one of the most frequently mutated tumor suppressor genes in human cancer, _______.
  2. p53 causes cells with ______________ DNA to arrest their growth and to _________-____________.
  3. INK4 affects the activity of another tumor suppressor, the retinoblastoma protein, RB.
  4. Mutations and ___________ that affect the INK4a/ARF locus are very common in different forms of human cancer.
  5. When ARF is eliminated in mice, the mice develop ___________.
  6. When the mice were given the ARF ___________, the mouse cell lines stopped proliferating in a cancerous way.

 

Go to http://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2004/11/041130205441.htm

 

  1. A group of researchers has re-created the ___________ (the sequence of the A’s, G’s, T’s, and C’s of DNA) of the common ancestor of all placental mammals. 
  2. The project was led by David H. Haussler at the University of California, Santa Cruz, where which one of your teachers went to college?
  3. They sequenced the CFTR gene, which has been sequenced in a large number of _____________ (different kinds of animals).
  4. Efforts to extract DNA from _____________ generally have been disappointing because DNA molecules break down over time.  Instead, geneticists have to ________ the DNA sequences of past organisms using computers. 
  5. The challenge for Haussler and his colleagues was to determine how the DNA sequence of the common ancestor ____________ in each of the evolutionary lineages leading to current mammals.
  6. In the FOXP2 region, “the nucleotide is ____ for more than 300 million years, and then suddenly it’s A, just in the human lineage,” he said. “You can see it. That’s the excitement of documenting these dramatic events that can change the nature of an organism over evolutionary time.”

13.  By examining which individual nucleotides and sections of DNA have __________ and which have remained the _______, geneticists can draw conclusions about which parts of our DNA are essential to the functioning of life.

14.  In addition to the approximately 1.5 percent of the human genome that codes for _________, several percent more has been strongly conserved [stayed the same over time] and must be playing some role in DNA’s function. “Someday we are going to understand what some of these other noncoding elements in the middle of __________ and upstream from genes are doing,” Haussler said. “But most of this is terra incognita today.”

  1. According to Mr. Haussler’s quote above in #14, does this mean that introns are probably completely unnecessary to living things, or that that might have some function that we just don’t know yet?

 

Go to http://users.rcn.com/jkimball.ma.ultranet/BiologyPages/T/Transposons.html#Transposons_in_maize

 

16.  Transposons are segments of DNA that can move around to different ___________ in the genome of a single cell. In the process, they may

  • cause ______________
  • increase (or decrease) the __________ of DNA in the genome.

These mobile segments of DNA are sometimes called “____________ genes”

17.  The first transposons were discovered in the 1940s by _________________who worked with maize (Zea mays, called “_______” in the U.S.). She found that they were responsible for a variety of types of gene mutations, usually

  • ___________
  • deletions and
  • translocations

18.  In developing somatic tissues like corn kernels, a mutation (e.g., c) that alters color will be passed on to all the descendant cells. This produces the _______________ (different colored kernels)  pattern which is so prized in “Indian corn”. (Photo courtesy of Whalls Farms.)

19.  It took about _________ years for other scientists to fully appreciate the significance of Barbara McClintock’s discoveries. She was finally awarded a Nobel Prize in ___________.

 

20.  Retrotransposons move by a “_______________” mechanism but in contrast to the transposons described above, the copy is made of RNA, not DNA.

The RNA copies are then transcribed back into DNA — using a __________________ — and these are inserted into new locations in the genome.

Many retrotransposons have long terminal repeats (LTRs) at their ends that may contain over ________ base pairs in each.

About _____% of the entire human genome consists of retrotransposons.

21.  HIV-1

HIV-1 — the cause of ______— and other human retroviruses (e.g., HTLV-1, the human T-cell leukemia virus) behave like ___________________.

The RNA genome of HIV-1 contains a gene for

  • _____________________ and one for
  • integrase. The integrase serves the same function as the transposases of DNA ___________________. The DNA copies can be inserted anywhere in the genome.

Molecules of both enzymes are incorporated in the ______________ particle.
