Biology Honors Syllabus

August 31, 2010 Copernicus Uncategorized

Course Syllabus: Biology Honors

Instructor: Ms. O’Connor; email:  oconnorh@galileoweb.org

Textbook: Miller & Levine, Biology

Course Description: Biology Honors will cover the key biological concepts as outlined in the State Science Standards. The two main goals of the course are to develop a conceptual framework for modern biology and to gain experience and appreciation of biology through experimentation and inquiry. Students will participate in projects which will develop the following skills: writing, critical thinking, technology usage, research, time management, cooperation, and presentation skills.

Grading: Students will earn grades according to the following system:

Tests: 60%

Homework: 15%

Classwork: 15%

Labs: 10%

Participation: Extra Credit

Grades are posted on School Loop.  Most assignments are on galileoweb.org/oconnorh.  Ms. O’Connor is available for tutoring on Mondays after school from 3:10-4:40 in Room 450 or lunchtime by appointment.

Expectations: Students are expected to:

1. Be on time every day.

2. Bring all required materials, which include the completed homework assignment, a laboratory notebook, pen, pencil, and some loose binder paper. In addition, sometimes you will be asked to bring your textbook to class. Be respectful of themselves, their fellow students, and their teacher by

a. Listening to students or teachers when they are speaking to the class.

b. Listening carefully to all directions, especially laboratory directions to prevent accidents to themselves and others.

c. Asking for help from fellow students and teachers when necessary.

As this is an Honors course, students will write essays, read science articles, do research and write laboratory reports which go beyond the traditional sophomore biology assignments.

Topics of the Course:

—1st Semester—-¬¬¬¬¬¬¬¬¬¬¬¬¬¬¬¬

Week 1-3 Introductions, Measurement, Scientific Method, Basic Biochemistry

Week 4-9 Cells – Cell Structure, Cell Transport, Enzymes

Week 10-11 Respiration & Photosynthesis

Week 12-16 Genetics

Week 17-18 Genetics Project; Review for Final Exam

—2nd Semester—-

Week 19-22 Evolution

Week 23-24 Ecology

Week 25-27 Human Physiology: Overview, Digestion and Nutrition, Skeletal and Muscular, Endocrine, Nervous, Immune, Reproductive

Week 28-29 Review for CST Biology Test; CST testing

Weeks 30-36 Classification of the 6 Kingdoms and major Phyla

Biology Assignment: Food Lab Write-Up

August 30, 2010 Copernicus Uncategorized

Lab – Testing Foods for Different Macromolecules

Write the following on separate paper:

Purpose: To see which foods had the following macromolecules: carbohydrates (including glucose and s__________), p______________, and f_____.

Materials: [list the materials we used]

Procedure: [list the steps we took in the lab]

Data: [copy your group’s data table]

Results: [write in complete sentences]
[Which foods turned green, yellow, orange or red with Benedict’s?
Which foods turned purple with Biuret?
Which foods turned black with Iodine?
Which foods made the paper translucent (light shined through)?]

Conclusions: [write in complete sentences]
[List the foods that had glucose.
List the foods that had starch.
List the foods that had protein.
List the foods that had fat.]

According to the Turtle Biology book page 34, carbohydrates play a key role in the storing and transporting of __________ in your body. Glucose is a good source of energy in a “short burst,” while starch will provide energy over a longer period of time. Which is better to eat while running a marathon, glucose or starch? Which foods that we tested would be good to eat the night before running a marathon? Why?

[Copy the diagram of the glucose molecule from page 34. Copy the diagram of starch from page 34.]

Starch is made of many repeating ____________ molecules (page 34).

According to the Turtle Biology book page 35, fats are __________-storage lipid molecules. A gram of fat provides ____ calories of energy, whereas a gram of carbohydrates provides only four calories. If your body needed a lot of energy, which of the foods that we tested would you eat? Why?

According to the Turtle Biology book page 35, proteins can have important _____________ functions, or some of them are enzymes that assist the chemical reactions of __________________. According to page 36, what are the building blocks of proteins? If you want to have healthy hair and muscles, which of the foods that we tested should you eat? Why?

Describe three errors that could have occurred in the lab which could have affected your results.

Powers of 10 website and Chemistry Internet Questions

PART 1

Go to: http://micro.magnet.fsu.edu/primer/java/scienceopticsu/powersof10/index.html

The pictures show you what the Earth looks like as you move in by a factor or power of 10 at a time.

Questions: You do NOT have to copy the questions. Answer these questions in your notebook.

1.What do you see at  

a. 1022 meters?

b. 1013 meters?

c. 108 meters?

d. 106 meters?

e. 101 meters?

f. 10-1 meters?

g. 10-5 meters?

h. 10-8 meters?

i. 10-14 meters?

 Some answers to #1 include: DNA, nucleus of an atom, tree, Earth, our solar system, stomata (holes on a leaf), the Milky Way, leaf, Florida state

Read the information on the website: http://micro.magnet.fsu.edu/primer/java/scienceopticsu/powersof10/index.html

2.Write how wide Earth is in exponential notation.

3.Write how wide a plant cell is in exponential notation.

4.What tools do scientists use to observe tiny (small) things? (Name two things).

5.What tools do scientists use to observe things that are far away? (Name one thing).

6.Who was the architect who originally advanced the Powers of Ten concept?

Part 3:  Mixtures

2.  Give an example of a solid-solid mix and a solid-fluid mix.

3. What is a colloidal mixture?

4. What is a solution?

5. What does dissolving mean?

6. List some non-polar molecules.

7. How can solutions be separated into solutes and solvents?
